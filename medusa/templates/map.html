THE LAW
Until 1993 Marital rape was legal in at least one state. North Carolina was the last to criminalize it — July 5, 1993. Until 1976, every single state had a spousal exemption allowing a husband to rape his wife with no legal consequences.
Still today Some states still treat marital rape differently than stranger rape — shorter reporting windows, reduced penalties, or additional force requirements. South Carolina requires proof of a weapon or extreme violence to prosecute spousal rape.
Child marriage Child marriage is still legal in 34 states. California, Mississippi, New Mexico, and Oklahoma set no minimum age — a child of any age can marry with parental consent. Nearly 315,000 children, mostly girls, were legally married in the U.S. between 2000 and 2021.
Marriage as escape In most states, marriage is a legal defense to statutory rape. Marrying a child you sexually abused makes the prior abuse legal. At least 66,000 child marriages since 2000 involved an age gap that would otherwise be a sex crime.
Until 1974 Women could not get a credit card in their own name. The Equal Credit Opportunity Act was not passed until 1974.
Until 1973 Women could be legally fired for being pregnant. The Pregnancy Discrimination Act did not pass until 1978.
Until 1920 Women could not vote. The 19th Amendment was ratified August 18, 1920 — 144 years after the nation was founded.
Until 1839 No state allowed women to own property in their own name. Mississippi was the first — and required a husband’s permission.

Sources: Unchained At Last · OJP · CDC · National Women’s History Alliance · Congress.gov
